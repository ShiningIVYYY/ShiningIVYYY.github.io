2. Introduction

2.1 Background & Business Context

Survival Analysis is a collection of statistical methods used to examine and predict the time until an event of interest occurs. Originally developed in healthcare for time-to-death research, it has since been widely adopted across industries. In the telecommunications sector, the event of interest is customer churn ¡ª the cancellation of a service subscription.

This analysis uses the IBM Telco Customer Churn dataset, which represents a fictitious Telecommunications company. It serves three key business use cases:

• Customer Retention: Predict at what point specific customers are likely to churn, enabling proactive retention actions.

• Hardware Failure Prediction: Predict when network hardware may need repair or replacement.

• Device & Plan Upgrade Timing: Predict when customers are likely to change their service plan.

 

2.2 Why Survival Analysis? ¡ª The Censoring Problem

A critical advantage of Survival Analysis over standard regression is its ability to handle censored data ¡ª observations where the event has not yet occurred by the end of the study period.

4. Kaplan-Meier Analysis

4.1 Method Overview

Kaplan-Meier (KM) is a non-parametric method for estimating survival probability curves. It correctly accounts for censored observations, overcoming the underestimation that occurs when using simple mean or median tenure. KM is primarily used for univariate analysis ¡ª one covariate at a time.

The model was fit using the Lifelines library with the following inputs:

• Duration column: tenure

• Event observed column: churn (1 = event, 0 = censored)

• Total observations: 3,351  |  Events: 1,556  |  Right-censored: 1,795

5. Cox Proportional Hazards Model

5.1 Method Overview

Cox Proportional Hazards (Cox PH) is a semi-parametric regression model for multi-variate survival analysis. It estimates the hazard ratio ¡ª the relative instantaneous risk of churn ¡ª for each covariate, while adjusting for all other variables simultaneously. The core assumption is that hazard ratios between individuals remain constant over time (proportional hazards).

8.3 Model Limitations

• Analysis is restricted to Month-to-Month contract customers ¡ª findings may differ for One-Year and Two-Year contract holders.

• 3 out of 4 variables in the Cox PH model violated the Proportional Hazards assumption; hazard ratio estimates are approximate for those variables over time.

• The AFT model (Log-Logistic) predicts a median survival of 135.51 months, which is much higher than the KM-estimated 34 months ¡ª this reflects the full population extrapolation beyond the 72-month observation window and parametric distributional assumptions.

• Dataset is synthetic (IBM sample data); real-world results may differ in magnitude.

• No time-varying covariates were modeled; service plan upgrades/downgrades during a customer's lifecycle are not captured.

• CLV calculations assume a flat $30/month profit ¡ª actual margins vary by customer segment and plan.

 


9. Conclusions

This report has documented the end-to-end Survival Analysis pipeline applied to Telco customer churn, based on five Databricks notebooks. The following conclusions are drawn from the recorded results:

• The Kaplan-Meier estimator established that the median Month-to-Month customer survives 34 months, with approximately 10% still active at 72 months. Ten out of fifteen tested covariates showed statistically significant survival differences.

• The Cox Proportional Hazards model identified four significant churn drivers: having dependents (HR=0.72), using DSL over Fiber optic (HR=0.80), having Online Backup (HR=0.46), and having Tech Support (HR=0.53). Online Backup is the strongest single retention lever.

• Three of four Cox PH variables violated the proportional hazards assumption, confirmed by both statistical tests and Schoenfeld residual plots. This motivates the use of the AFT model for individual-level predictions.

• The AFT (Log-Logistic) model achieved a higher concordance index (0.73 vs 0.64) and significantly better AIC (13,699 vs 22,640), confirming it as the superior predictive model for this dataset. All nine add-on service and payment features significantly extend customer survival time.

• The CLV dashboard operationalizes the Cox PH survival curves to calculate NPV across a 36-month horizon. For a DSL customer on a $30/month plan with default settings, the cumulative NPV is $251.40 at 12 months, $405.44 at 24 months, and $415.76 at 36 months.
